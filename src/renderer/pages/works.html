Noli Me Tangere

üìÖ Published: February 21, 1887 üìç Berlin, Germany üìñ Genre: Social Novel

"Noli Me Tangere" (Touch Me Not) is Rizal's groundbreaking first novel that exposed the corruption, abuse, and injustices of Spanish colonial rule in the Philippines. Written in Spanish and published in Berlin with help from Maximo Viola, this masterpiece follows Crisostomo Ibarra's return from Europe and his discovery of the tragic fate of his father and the deep-seated injustices plaguing Filipino society. The novel was immediately banned in the Philippines but circulated widely, awakening Filipino national consciousness.

Key Themes:

  • Colonial oppression and abuse of power by Spanish friars and officials
  • Social inequality and rigid class divisions in Philippine society
  • The transformative power of education as key to national progress
  • Religious hypocrisy and the gap between Christian teachings and clergy behavior
  • Filipino identity and the call for national consciousness and pride
  • The destruction of Filipino families under colonial oppression

Plot Summary:

The novel begins with a lavish dinner party hosted by Capitan Tiago, where Crisostomo Ibarra returns after seven years in Europe. He learns that his father Don Rafael died in prison, branded as a heretic and denied a Christian burial by Padre D√°maso. Ibarra plans to honor his father by building a school, but faces opposition from the friars who see education as a threat to their power.

Noli Me Tangere was immediately banned by Spanish authorities in the Philippines, but copies circulated secretly, inspiring the reform movement and later the Philippine Revolution. The novel's exposure of colonial abuses awakened Filipino national consciousness and is considered one of the catalysts for Philippine independence.

El Filibusterismo

üìÖ Published: September 18, 1891 üìç Ghent, Belgium üìñ Genre: Political Novel

"El Filibusterismo" (The Reign of Greed) is the darker, more politically charged sequel to Noli Me Tangere. Set thirteen years later, it follows Simoun (Ibarra in disguise) who has abandoned peaceful reform for revenge and revolution. Dedicated to GOMBURZA (the three martyred Filipino priests), this novel explores the failure of peaceful reform and the temptation of violent revolution, ultimately questioning whether violence can ever lead to true justice. The Spanish authorities used this novel as evidence against Rizal during his trial.

Key Themes:

  • Revolution versus reform - the tension between violent uprising and peaceful change
  • Corruption and moral decay in advanced stages throughout society
  • The failure of education and assimilation under colonial oppression
  • Revenge and its destructive consequences on the soul
  • Sacrifice, martyrdom, and the price of seeking justice
  • Greed and materialism as corrupting forces ("The Reign of Greed")

Main Characters:

Simoun - The mysterious jeweler who is actually Crisostomo Ibarra in disguise. Wealthy and influential, he has returned after 13 years seeking revenge. He manipulates the corrupt system to hasten its collapse, believing violent revolution is the only solution. Represents disillusionment with peaceful reform.
Basilio - Sisa's son who survived the events of Noli Me Tangere. Now a medical student, he represents the new generation of educated Filipinos. Despite his tragic past, he still believes in working within the system for gradual change.
Isagani - An idealistic poet and Basilio's friend, engaged to Paulita G√≥mez. He represents youthful idealism and the struggle between love and principle. His character embodies the conflict between personal happiness and national duty.
Paulita G√≥mez - Isagani's fianc√©e who ultimately chooses wealth over love by marrying the wealthy Juanito Pel√°ez. Represents Filipinos who prioritize material comfort over principles and sacrifice.
Padre Florentino - A wise and compassionate Filipino priest who serves as the novel's moral compass. He counsels Simoun in his final moments, delivering the novel's ultimate message about the futility of revenge and the need for moral regeneration.
Kabesang Tales - A farmer driven to banditry after losing his land to the friars. His story represents the desperation of the Filipino masses and how oppression creates outlaws and revolutionaries.
Juli - Tales' daughter who sacrifices herself to save her father. She chooses death over dishonor, symbolizing Filipino women's suffering and the ultimate price of colonial oppression.
Placido Penitente - A student who represents the youth's frustration with the educational system. His experiences show how colonial education stifles rather than enlightens Filipino minds.

Memorable Quote:

"He who would love much has also much to suffer."
"I die without seeing the dawn brighten over my native land. You, who have it to see, welcome it‚Äîand forget not those who have fallen during the night!"

Plot Summary:

Thirteen years after the events of Noli Me Tangere, Simoun has returned to the Philippines as a wealthy jeweler and confidant of the Captain-General. He uses his influence to encourage corruption and abuse, believing that worsening conditions will drive the people to revolution. He plans to use a wedding celebration to detonate a bomb that will kill the country's elite and spark an uprising.

Meanwhile, Basilio struggles to complete his medical studies while dealing with the trauma of his past. Isagani fights for educational reforms and loves Paulita G√≥mez, but she abandons him for a wealthier suitor. Kabesang Tales loses his land to the friars and becomes a bandit, while his daughter Juli sacrifices herself to save him.

Simoun's plot is foiled when Isagani, despite his own grievances, prevents the bombing to save innocent lives. Wounded and dying, Simoun flees to Padre Florentino's house. In his final moments, the priest counsels him that true freedom cannot be won through violence and bloodshed, but must be earned through suffering and moral regeneration. Simoun dies, and Padre Florentino throws his jewels into the sea, symbolizing the rejection of materialism and violence as paths to liberation.

Historical Impact:

El Filibusterismo presented a darker, more pessimistic view than its predecessor, reflecting Rizal's growing disillusionment with Spanish colonial reform. The novel was used as evidence against Rizal during his trial, with authorities claiming it incited rebellion. Its exploration of the limits of peaceful reform and the temptation of violence made it a controversial but influential work in Philippine revolutionary thought.

The novel is dedicated to the memory of the three martyred Filipino priests‚ÄîFathers Mariano G√≥mez, Jos√© Burgos, and Jacinto Zamora‚Äîwho were executed in 1872. Their unjust deaths profoundly influenced Rizal's nationalist awakening and inspired his commitment to Philippine reform and independence.
